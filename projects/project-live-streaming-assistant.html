Live-Streaming Assistant

Streaming Assistant is an AI-assisted live streaming companion system designed to help streamers manage real-time audience interaction, emotional pressure, and cultural communication risks during broadcasts. The product is an AI-assisted live-streaming companion system that combines a camera w/ display stand, a pressure-sensitive footpad, microphone input, LED reminders, and AI analysis to help streamers manage audience interaction without breaking their performance flow. The concept is based on three real-time input channels: audience comment analysis, streamer monitoring, and foot-controlled interaction. The project combines Human-Computer Interaction, industrial design and AI-based feedback systems to create a low-intervention “backstage assistant” that supports streamers without interrupting their natural performance. The system includes a facial recognition camera, OLED feedback display, microphone, LED reminder strip, and a pressure-sensitive footpad interface. While the camera and microphone monitor the streamer’s facial expression and speech, the AI analyzes live comments, audience sentiment, trending keywords, and potential cultural risks. Instead of forcing the streamer to constantly read fast-moving chat messages, the system summarizes key information into subtle visual cues and real-time suggestions. A key interaction feature is the foot-controlled pad, which allows streamers to trigger quick actions without breaking eye contact with the camera. Through subtle foot movements, the streamer can activate functions such as topic generation, sentiment analysis, quick polls, mini-games, warm-up interactions, or blocking disruptive viewers. This makes the system especially useful during cold moments, audience drop-offs, hostile comments, or moments when the streamer needs fast support while staying visually present on camera. The design responds to several pain points found through background research and interviews: streamers often struggle with visual overload, delayed audience feedback, emotional vulnerability, interaction stagnation, and the pressure of managing performance and chat at the same time. The final concept positions AI not as a replacement for the streamer, but as a quiet support layer that improves control, awareness, and confidence while preserving authenticity and personal expression. The proposed system operates as an AI-assisted livestreaming companion designed to support streamers throughout the full broadcasting process, beginning before the livestream formally starts. Prior to going live, the streamer configures two primary hardware modules within the streaming environment: a visual sensing and feedback module, and a hidden foot-based interaction module. The visual sensing and feedback module is positioned near the main camera, monitor, or streaming desk, where it can continuously observe the streamer and provide glanceable feedback without interrupting the performance. This module integrates a facial recognition camera, an OLED feedback display, a microphone, an LED light strip, a telescopic central rod, and a foldable stand structure. In parallel, the foot interaction module is placed beneath the desk or near the streamer’s chair, allowing the user to access control functions through subtle foot movements. This footpad contains a soft blanket layer, LED light strip, intermediate support plate, pressure-sensitive sensor zones, a main control board, a base plate, and mounting components. Together, these two modules establish a distributed interaction system in which sensing, analysis, feedback, and control are separated across visible and hidden interfaces. Once the livestream begins, the system enters a continuous real-time monitoring state. During this stage, multiple information streams are captured simultaneously from the live broadcasting environment. The first stream is audience-generated data, collected through the live platform’s barrage or comment API. This channel captures real-time chat messages, audience questions, emojis, topic keywords, repeated comments, spam, hostile language, and other viewer reactions. The second stream is visual data, collected through the camera, which tracks the streamer’s facial landmarks, eye movement, expression changes, attention state, and visible emotional condition. The third stream is audio data, collected through the microphone, which monitors the streamer’s speech rhythm, vocal tone, pacing, pauses, clarity, and potential hesitation. These three streams are then transmitted into an AI analysis layer, where the system interprets both audience-side signals and streamer-side performance conditions. Rather than treating livestreaming as a single-direction content delivery process, the system understands it as a dynamic feedback environment shaped by audience response, streamer behavior, platform rhythm, and contextual risk. The first major layer of AI processing focuses on audience input analysis. In this layer, real-time comments are processed through sentiment analysis, topic extraction, keyword frequency detection, engagement monitoring, and risk classification. The system evaluates whether the overall audience mood is positive, neutral, confused, bored, hostile, excited, or unstable. It also identifies whether specific topics are becoming increasingly popular, whether the same question is being repeated by multiple viewers, whether the audience is losing interest, or whether the discussion atmosphere is becoming inactive. This process transforms fragmented chat messages into more structured and interpretable feedback. Instead of requiring the streamer to manually scan fast-moving comments, the system summarizes audience behavior into visual and textual outputs such as emotion curves, keyword clouds, trending topic lists, engagement level indicators, and risk signals. These outputs allow the streamer to understand the collective condition of the audience more efficiently, especially in situations where comment volume is too high, too chaotic, or too emotionally charged to interpret manually. The second AI layer focuses on streamer-state monitoring. This layer evaluates whether the streamer’s current behavior, emotional expression, and speaking style are appropriate for the livestreaming context. By combining facial recognition data and voice analysis, the system can identify signs that the streamer may appear tired, confused, overly serious, emotionally unstable, distracted, frustrated, or disengaged. It can also detect communication issues such as speaking too quickly, over-explaining a topic, leaving excessive silent gaps, missing important audience comments, or using expressions that may not match the audience’s emotional state. This function is especially relevant in global or cross-cultural livestreaming contexts, where language, humor, gestures, facial expressions, and sensitive topics may be interpreted differently by audiences from diverse cultural backgrounds. In this sense, the system does not only analyze performance efficiency, but also supports cultural adaptability and communication safety. It helps the streamer maintain a more appropriate on-camera presence while reducing the risk of misunderstanding, offense, or audience disengagement. The third AI layer performs context matching between audience behavior and streamer performance. This layer compares the current condition of the audience with the streamer’s ongoing behavior in order to identify moments of mismatch, stagnation, or risk. For example, if audience activity is decreasing while the streamer continues a long monologue, the system can classify the situation as a possible stagnation moment. If viewers repeatedly ask similar questions but the streamer does not respond, the system can recognize delayed audience recognition. If hostile comments, spam, spoilers, or disruptive behavior appear in the chat, the system can identify a chat hygiene risk. If the streamer’s facial expression appears frustrated while the audience mood becomes negative, the system can detect emotional vulnerability. Similarly, if the streamer uses a phrase, joke, gesture, or topic that may be culturally sensitive, the system can mark it as a possible communication misalignment. Through this context-matching process, the system moves beyond simple data visualization and becomes an interpretive assistant that understands the relationship between audience atmosphere, streamer behavior, and livestream interaction flow. After identifying the current situation, the system determines what form of feedback should be delivered to the streamer. The feedback strategy is intentionally designed as low-intervention because the product is not intended to replace the streamer’s judgment or make the broadcast feel controlled by AI. Instead, it operates as a backstage assistant that provides discreet guidance while preserving the streamer’s authenticity, personality, and performative freedom. The OLED display presents concise and glanceable information, such as “viewer number is dropping,” “trolls detected,” “topic energy is low,” “inventory is running low,” “try a quick poll,” or “cultural risk detected.” In addition to text prompts, the display can show compact visual summaries, including audience emotion curves, keyword highlights, discussion heat levels, topic suggestions, and short response recommendations. This allows the streamer to quickly understand the problem without reading long paragraphs or breaking eye contact with the camera for an extended period. The LED light strip provides a second layer of nonverbal feedback. Compared with the OLED display, which communicates through text and data visualization, the LED strip communicates through light behavior, including brightness changes, soft pulses, quick flashes, or continuous glowing states. This allows the system to alert the streamer even when they cannot look directly at the display. For example, a soft pulse may indicate that audience attention is gradually decreasing, a quick flash may indicate that a risk has been detected, and a steady glow may indicate that the system is in normal monitoring mode. This feedback method is intentionally subtle so that it can support the streamer without being noticed by the audience. In the overall workflow, the LED strip functions as a fast-recognition channel, while the OLED screen functions as a more detailed interpretation channel. Together, they create a layered feedback structure that can shift from ambient awareness to more explicit guidance depending on the severity of the situation. The pressure-sensitive footpad functions as the primary control interface of the system. Since streamers often need to maintain eye contact with the camera while also managing keyboards, mice, product samples, game controllers, scripts, or performance tools, hand-based control can easily increase cognitive and physical workload. The footpad addresses this problem by providing a hidden input method that does not interrupt the visible performance. The pad is divided into multiple pressure-sensitive zones, and each zone can be assigned to a different AI-assisted function. For example, one zone may generate warm-up topics, another may launch a quick poll, another may activate a mini-game, another may block or remove disruptive viewers, and another may request a short sentiment summary or topic suggestion. Through this interaction method, the streamer can trigger system functions without visibly pausing, reaching for another device, or disrupting the flow of the livestream. The footpad therefore transforms the streamer’s lower-body movement into a discreet command layer, supporting multitasking while maintaining a natural frontstage appearance. A typical interaction cycle begins when the system detects a decline in audience engagement. For instance, if the comment frequency decreases, viewer activity drops, and the audience emotion curve shifts toward neutral or negative states, the AI identifies this as a possible interaction breakdown. The OLED display then presents a short warning indicating that viewership or engagement is dropping, while the LED strip provides a subtle visual cue. The streamer can respond by pressing a designated footpad zone, which prompts the system to generate an interaction strategy such as a warm-up question, a quick poll, a topic transition, or a short audience engagement prompt. The suggested content appears on the OLED display, allowing the streamer to naturally incorporate it into the conversation. After the intervention, the AI continues monitoring comment frequency, sentiment shifts, and audience response to evaluate whether the strategy was effective. If the audience mood improves, the system returns to passive monitoring. If the problem continues, the system can escalate to stronger prompts or suggest alternative interaction actions. Another important scenario is hostile chat management. In this situation, the barrage or comment API detects trolls, spam, spoilers, aggressive comments, or repeated disruptive behavior. The AI classifies these patterns as chat hygiene risks and presents the streamer with a concise moderation prompt on the OLED screen, such as whether disruptive viewers should be removed. Instead of manually searching through the chat or pausing the stream to manage moderation settings, the streamer can press a confirmation zone on the footpad. The system then sends a moderation command to block, remove, or restrict the disruptive users. After the action is completed, the OLED screen displays a confirmation message, such as “The trolls have been kicked out,” and the system returns to continuous monitoring mode. This interaction reduces the emotional and cognitive burden placed on the streamer, especially during moments when hostile comments may interrupt their rhythm or affect their emotional state. A third scenario involves cultural sensitivity and communication adjustment. In global livestreaming contexts, streamers may interact with audiences from different linguistic, cultural, and social backgrounds. A phrase, joke, gesture, or topic that seems harmless in one context may be misunderstood or considered inappropriate in another. When the AI detects a possible cultural or linguistic risk, it does not interrupt the streamer with a loud warning. Instead, the LED strip provides a discreet alert, and the OLED screen shows a short warning or alternative phrasing suggestion. The streamer can then soften their expression, clarify their meaning, rephrase a sentence, or transition away from the topic. This feature supports more inclusive communication while preserving the streamer’s natural style. Rather than enforcing rigid behavioral control, the system provides situational awareness and optional correction, allowing the streamer to remain responsible for the final communication decision. The system can also support commerce-oriented livestreaming. In this context, the AI monitors audience questions, product mentions, purchase hesitation, inventory status, and repeated concerns about price, size, shipping, discount availability, restocking, or product comparison. If many viewers ask similar product-related questions, the OLED display can summarize the most frequent topics so that the streamer can respond more efficiently. If the product inventory becomes low, the system can display a restock reminder or suggest that the streamer mention scarcity as part of the sales rhythm. Through the footpad, the streamer can trigger a product explanation, launch a quick Q&A, display a restock message, or generate a purchase-related interaction prompt without interrupting the flow of presentation. In this case, the system supports both audience engagement and commercial performance, but still maintains the principle of low-intervention assistance. Overall, the workflow of the product is structured as a continuous feedback loop: observe, analyze, compare, warn, suggest, trigger, confirm, and monitor again. The system first observes the livestream through audience comments, facial expression data, and voice input. It then analyzes audience sentiment, topic trends, engagement levels, streamer state, and potential communication risks. After that, it compares audience condition with streamer behavior in order to detect mismatches, stagnation, hostile activity, emotional vulnerability, or cultural sensitivity issues. Based on the severity of the situation, the system provides layered feedback through LED alerts, OLED text, visual summaries, and foot-triggered action options. After the streamer responds, the AI evaluates whether audience mood, comment frequency, viewer retention, or streamer expression improves. If the intervention succeeds, the system returns to quiet monitoring. If the issue continues, it escalates from subtle ambient signals to more explicit suggestions and interactive options. Through this layered response structure, the product positions AI as a supportive backstage system rather than a visible replacement for human performance, helping streamers maintain engagement, emotional stability, cultural awareness, and interaction control during complex real-time broadcasts.
