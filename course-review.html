Course Guide

I use this page to keep notes on courses I have taken at UW-Madison, unless otherwise specified. I am pursuing a B.S. in Computer Sciences and a B.S. in Information Science, so yeah… there are also a lot of random breadth classes here lol.

Table of Contents

Course Number Course Title Semester Instructor

COMP SCI 200: Programming I

Instructor: Andrew Kuemmel | TA: Christian Aidan Classen

3 credits course introduces the fundamental concepts of Java, including definitions and terminology, loops, data structures, and recursion. I could have taken the placement test and gone directly into CS 300, but due to a series of unpredictable incidents, I chose not to. Looking back, I do not regret that decision.The course includes two midterms, two projects, and one final exam in total. On a weekly basis, there are usually three labs, one lecture, and three homework assignments. It is not a very difficult course. From my perspective, it is a well transitioned and streamlined class for students who are new to programming. Students are encouraged to find a partner during labs. We use Runestone to complete homework and ZyBooks to do exercises. I learned most of the material during labs, where the instructor gives students the autonomy to solve problems by themselves with assistance from the TAs. This is an efficient way for us to learn and actually remember things in the long term.

ECE 252: Introduction to Computer Engineering

Instructor: Shayne Wadle

This was comparatively the most challenging course I took this semester. It introduced the fundamentals of computer architecture, which I was completely unfamiliar with before taking the class. The course covers arithmetic calculations involving hexadecimal, decimal, and binary numbers, as well as assembly code and machine code. Some parts were pretty abstract and took me a while to figure out, especially 32-bit RISC-V machine code. There are so many variations, and each one requires accurate calculation to get the correct answer. The course includes four midterms, eight homework assignments, and four projects. There are two lectures every week, but attendance is not mandatory. I can honestly say that without help from my classmates, I probably could not have achieved an A.

ESL 116: Academic Reading and Vocabulary Skills

Instructor: Professor Alejandro Azocar

Over the semester, I think we reviewed more than fifteen medium-length articles filled with our annotations. We also learned how to write selective summary, integrated summary and make citation correctly. Professor Azocar is a kind, easy-going, and passionate teacher. He told me that he would travel to Hong Kong to attend a conference and then fly to Italy for a month to learn Italian. He has such a positive and optimistic attitude toward life, and I like him very much. We had several instructive conversations that helped me a lot with adapting to a new environment. There were three vocabulary exams and multiple summary tests, which were counted toward the final grade. By the way, if you do well in this course, namely you achieve over 92% for the final, you will have the opportunity to skip ESL 117 and go directly into ESL 118, which saves both money and time.

AF 100: Introduction to African Cultural Expression

Instructor: Xi Jin

Super chill class! An introductory course for learning about different countries and cultures in Africa. Each week focused on a different theme, usually connected to a specific African country. However, there were also some weeks that introduced broader topics, such as African pop music and podcasts, without focusing on one particular country. To be frank, getting an A in this course is very manageable. The course includes one midterm, one final, weekly essays, and one presentation, and that is basically all. Our instructor is a very easy-going Chinese PhD candidate. She was quite generous with grading. Overall, the workload was not heavy and the grading was very friendly.

SW 100: So You Want To Change The World?

Instructor: Yolanda Shelton-Morris

This course was not the most engaging course for me personally. First of all, attendance is required for each class! And it is counted toward the final grade, even though the class meets at 8 A.M., which honestly feels like inhumane torture. In each class, Yo mainly read the slides aloud, which made everyone sleepy. Sometimes, she would also organize group interaction activities, but most of them were not-so-interesting. The final grade is based on 15 points for attendance, four essays worth 15 points each, and one final essay worth 25 points. To be frank, the grading of the essays felt extremely subjective. My suggestion is to avoid taking this course!
